WHERE TO GO
Florence, the luminous heart of Tuscany, is a city where the past feels vividly alive. Every street seems to echo with the footsteps of artists, philosophers, and visionaries who helped shape the Renaissance. The iconic dome of the Florence Cathedral rises above terracotta rooftops, a masterpiece of engineering and imagination that still inspires awe centuries after Brunelleschi first conceived it. Along the Arno River, the Ponte Vecchio glitters with goldsmiths’ workshops, their tradition carried on through generations.
Rome is a city where layers of history rise and overlap like pages in a living chronicle. At its heart stands the Colosseum, an enduring symbol of ancient grandeur, where the roar of crowds and the clash of gladiators seem almost within reach. Nearby, the Roman Forum stretches out as a maze of temples, arches, and pathways that once formed the center of an empire. But Rome is far more than its ancient stones—it's Renaissance palaces, Baroque fountains, and narrow cobblestone streets add warmth and character to the city’s grand narrative.
Tuscany is a land where beauty unfolds at every turn, a region shaped by time, art, and nature working in quiet harmony. Rolling hills ripple across the countryside, brushed with silvery olive groves and endless rows of vineyards that glow gold and green under the sun. Medieval towns rise from the landscape like living paintings, their stone walls and terracotta roofs holding centuries of stories. Tuscany’s true wonder lies not only in its sights, but in the feeling it leaves behind: a timeless sense of warmth, artistry, and reverence for life’s quiet pleasures.
Venice is a city born of water and imagination, a place where reality feels gently suspended between sea and sky. Canals replace streets, carrying gondolas that glide past weathered palaces whose facades shimmer in the shifting light. Stone bridges arch gracefully over the water, each one a threshold to another quiet marvel. Venice’s wonders are found in reflections, in the hush of lapping tides, and in the sense that the city itself is a fragile masterpiece—enduring, romantic, and unlike anywhere else in the world.
